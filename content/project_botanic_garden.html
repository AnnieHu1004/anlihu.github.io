St Andrews Botanic Garden Database Portal

A holobiont-style database web portal
that can mutualize visitors, researchers and managers together.

Project Vision

This project provides possible solutions for users in a range of formats to access St Andrews Botanic Garden's database with pertinence and efficiency. With current database management system our client is using, it has limitations in nearly every aspect and is extremely outdated for large datasets. Taking holobint as a core concept, our designed portals aim to provide a platform that can benefit both specialists and the general public to interact with data.

Challenges

1) Design an easy-to-use data processing system
2) Include a mapping capability to interrogate the database
3) Consider mobile functionalities to complement the website
4) Balance advanced capabilities with a user experience suitable for more than just expert users

Kickoff: Visting The Garden

We teamed up together with St Andrews Botanic Garden in their pursuit to refresh the scientific mission and overall purpose for professionals and visitors alike. However, at the outset of the project, we didn’t have a clear vision or specific goals about what we gonna build. Without pre-existing insights, we decided to visit the garden in person to get an idea of what this garden feels like.

Due to requirements from the university, we could not interview our client in person during the pandemic. Even so, this visit provided us with a perfect chance to explore the garden as a pure visitor, allowing us to find out how to work with data and information in the garden.

Define User Goals and Elicitate Insights

We grouped target users into three different user roles. The workflow on the right also shows how the information passes through different roles inside and outside the Botanic Garden, aiding us in identifying how the entities within the project system will communicate and coordinate with each other.

1. Manager

People are responsible for managing the plants, the science and research associated with them and looking after the database.

2. General Staff

People who do not directly amend data in the database but use it in their work and submit update suggestions through the portal.

3. Outsider

People who interact with the Botanic Garden database by only accessing data.

Bear Our Users in Mind

Having constructed three unique User Work Roles and a comprehensive Flow Model based on the data gathered, we decided to approach analysing the needs and challenges of the project from one final direction: through User Personas. These were designed to contrast the very specific and mechanical user work roles, and to essentially get an outside-the-box understanding of the actual users of the web portal. ‍

The User Personas were quite un-orthodoxically not designed to inform us of specific requirements and design choices of the final project, but rather to challenge the team to look at the problem from a more personal and empathetic perspective.

While there are multiple users and stakeholders involved with the Web Portal, and there is limited time to implement the project, we mainly limited our attention to the user experience of the managers and general staff. As the highest priority users of the web portal, ensuring they have smooth and seamless experiences with data queries and manipulations, the outsider can also benefit from this.

To help us visualise and communicate our areas of interest, we sketched storyboards first, which described and reminded us of the what, when, where and how.

Sketches

"Design interfaces for a database, instead of designing a database!"

Our job is to design a web portal to complement the new improved database that the Garden is developing instead of designing a database itself. Bearing that in mind and considering the scenarios depicted in storyboards, we applied our ideas and the client’s wishes from the mood board and user research insights to create concept sketches.

As the client did not need us to come up with a prototype that can be directly delivered to developers, what he wanted from us is to provide different possibilities to inspire him for future design and development. Hence, we focused on essential modules, including data manipulation, message receiving and handling, interactive map and mobile complement, instead of the whole website.

Challenge 01 Data Processing System

Goal 01: Highest Permission

Once managers have logged onto the portal, they have full access to the dataset to search, insert, delete, and update all data。

The administrator portal and its features are essentially a collection of the most important features we derived from our analysis.

*This page has notably been designed as low-fidelity grayscale wireframes to represent intentions and functionalities rather than colours or detailed visuals.

*Different users have different levels of access, but due to time limitations and priority considerations, we only prototyped the interface for the full-access manager.

Goal 02: Easy Data Manipulation

The managers can easily select the data they need to use and sort data according to their requirements with few clicks. The switch button for edit mode could reduce the likelihood of user misuse of data.

As for adding new data, users no longer have to drag and drop excel tables to check data column by column. They can fill in the form or import data in bulk.

Goal 03: Request Handling

The Request option allows the administrator to manage requests sent by other staff members who do not have permission to edit data directly.

The "Warming" status will alert administrators that there are operations or requests on the system that do not comply with database security or rules.

Goal 04: No Specific Skills Required

Prototypes shown above do not need specific skills to use, users can naturally transit from Excel to current system.

However, we also provide a Script option, allowing a user to load in R or Python scripts to process the data in a predetermined and automated way. This integration can not only serve better for high-level users, but also bridge the gap between straightforward functionality and powerful functionality that was communicated from curator questionnaire responses during data elicitation.

Goal 05: Track Data Source

The Diary option accesses a page of the history of changes made across the system and points every change to corresponding data operators. This feature came about when exploring how to keep track of all the minor but possibly impactful daily modifications made in the portal. The notion is that the feature would relieve the users’ cognitive load and thus aid the overall workflow.

Challenge 02 A mapping capability to interrogate the database

Goal 06: Easy-to-Use Interactive Map

In this map, plants would not only be locatable on the map, but one could also work with the data while using the map and hopping between different levels of viewing data seamlessly. The vivid information card can both benefit offline and online users.

Goal 07: Different Access Level to Data

The save button above is designed to be a feature for both professional and general users. It acts as a sort of short-term memory and enhances the ease of use. This idea primarily arose when we considered the scenario of a remote researcher who doesn't have an authorised account to download data in bulk searching for multiple plants. This saved tab feature helps relieve unnecessary cognitive load for any user.

Goal 08: Powerful Search Engine

When a user searches for a particular plant, ambiguous field or plant ID on the web portal, the results page is reached.

The page contrasts with the more specialised administrator pages above. It is designed to show an accessible and intuitive mode of browsing search results. It is in line with the information derived from the analysis of the elicited data, where it became apparent that visitors and lower-level users need simple but usable interfaces.

Challenge 03 Mobile Functionalities

Prototypes below showcase interfaces adapted for mobile users of the platform. These are all a part of our advanced features and are not as such a part of our most basic suggestions. Although our client mainly expressed their wishes about the web portal, considering the daily work scenarios of the staff, they are usually in the garden checking plants and updating the data in the database, designing a responsive webpage with special functions on the mobile would significantly improve work efficiency.

QR Code

Conveniently, using a phone’s camera like this is also a very fast method of searching and can thus be utilised by all user groups. Staff, who have logged in to the system, could scan QR codes to access and update data, while visitors could scan to explore plants they are interested in. ‍

The idea for QR codes came from the interviews with the client, where a question that arose was how to find a plant quickly without typing its long and complicated proper noun.

Image Cognition

While common and highly convenient, QR code nevertheless comes with the flaw of having to print out tags for each plant if used. ‍

As a complement method, we came up with the idea of using neural networks and image recognition to search the database by simply pointing your phone’s camera at a plant. For implementation, Google Lens already makes such a feat possible and could therefore be utilised.

Challenge 04 Create User Experience for More Than Just Expert Users

The Out-of-Box Idea

A question the team asked itself during the sketching process was how can we help the general visitor with little to no experience with plants? While the QR code and image recognition were possible for mobile users, the team wanted to find something similarly intuitive for desktop users as well.

We offered our client a possible solution for this. The intuitive search method, an out-of-box idea that aims to provide inspiration for the client instead of a function that can be developed right away, is based on visuals rather than scientific attributes. What size of a plant are you looking for? What colour is it? an choose between different physical attributes of the plant and as they make their choices, the image cards on the right-hand side update dynamically. The user can of course click on one of the cards and thus access more information about the plant.

Reflection

First, I want to thank my teammates as we've done great teamwork together. All of us were keen to design a prototype that met our client's requirements and exceeded their expectations, and we were all each other backup.

For me, the whole design process is very fulfilling. I applied what I had learned from the course to a practical project step by step, turning users' needs and insights we gathered from user searches into different prototypes. Although this project met the client's needs, it left much desired in terms of industry perspective. It consists of different modules without a well-established information architecture and is designed with three different software, and lacked a consistent design system due to time constraints. It does not mean I will just leave it there but incorporate them into my future projects.
